The art of calligraphy, particularly Nastaʿlīq script, which is the focus of this article,has undergone a long process of evolution over the centuries. Countless artists have dedicated years and countless hours of their lives to refining and elevating this script. This process has not merely been driven by personal taste or individual aesthetics; rather, it is grounded in a solid foundation of scientific principles, mathematical rules, and the visual fundamentals of art. Those who have contributed to the formation and growth of this art have possessed deep mastery of these principles and have strived with scholarly precision to advance it.

In this section, I will briefly touch upon some of the principles and rules of the Nastaʿlīq script. Nevertheless, it is worth noting that I hope to introduce and explore more dimensions of this noble art in the future through a series of continuous articles on this very page.

Unit of Measurement

To measure any phenomenon, the first step is to define a unit of measurement. In calligraphy, the width of the pen’s nib during writing is chosen as the standard unit. Since a reed pen (qalam) is carved in such a way that its tip has a flat edge, its first contact with the paper, after being dipped in ink, produces a linear mark, rather than a round dot like that of a typical ballpoint pen. This width, which varies depending on the type of pen from very fine to large sizes, forms the fundamental basis for determining the size and proportion of letters and words in Nastaʿlīq script. It is a clever choice that ensures proportionality remains aligned with both the tool and the scale of the work.

Picture 1: Unit of Measurement in Persian calligraphy

Based on this measurement standard, calligraphers over the centuries, drawing on experience, knowledge, continuous practice, and a deep understanding of aesthetic principles, have developed a rich set of precise rules for writing letters, forming words, and composing layouts. These rules have ultimately led to the creation of a coherent, harmonious, and visually captivating structure.

As shown in the image, the classical length of the letter Alef is considered to be three dots (Picture 2- N.1). In such cases, it is the calligrapher’s keen eye and aesthetic sensitivity that play a crucial role in maintaining the overall harmony.

Example 1: Examining the Letter “B”

In Nastaʿlīq script, this letter is written in two classical styles, each with its own specific writing rules. The first form is the around “five-dot B” (Picture 3). The second is the “extended B” (Picture 4), whose length, depending on the needs of the composition, is considered to range between 7 to 11 dots. The difference in size and writing style between these two forms has a significant impact on the visual balance and rhythm of the composition. The choice between them depends on the letter’s position within the word and the calligrapher’s artistic judgment.

Proportions Between Length and Width

It is important to note that the three dots mentioned in Example 1 must be proportionate to the thickness of the letter Alef itself. Therefore, the angle of the pen and the direction of its movement play a key role in correctly forming this proportion.

Picture 5: How the initial pen angle on paper affects the thickness of movement.

Initial Pen Angle on Paper

One of the key factors in the formation of letterforms is the angle at which the pen is placed at the start of movement. The very beginning of each stroke, like many decisions in life, plays a decisive role and can determine both the direction of the movement and the final outcome. At that moment, one can already sense: was the beginning made correctly? Will it lead to the form I envision, or not? This is because the angle directly affects the variations in thickness and delicacy of the letters, and forms the foundation of many of the visual contrasts in calligraphy. Achieving the ideal beginning is the result of mastering many subtle techniques, something attainable only through extensive practice. In this process, the correct way of holding the pen and the positioning of the calligrapher’s body in relation to the paper are essential details that directly influence movement control and the precise transfer of energy to the pen’s tip.

Picture 6: Holding the pen and the positioning of the calligrapher’s body in relation to the paper.

Energy Transmission

Energy transmission is, in essence, the transfer of passion, enthusiasm, love, or more precisely, the artist’s soul, through the pen and ink onto the surface of the paper. This phenomenon is a profound and thought-provoking aspect of the artistic creation process. The flow of energy must evolve and flourish alongside the learning of techniques, rules, and principles of any art form. Along this path, reflection on anthropology and self-knowledge is also considered a fundamental pillar in the cultivation of authentic art, because, ultimately, the question arises: Who is it that sets this pen in motion? And what is their purpose in doing so?

Direction of Movement

After a proper beginning, the essential question is: in which direction will the pen move? What is its path? Just like the course of life, this movement requires constant awareness at every moment. Full presence in the act of writing means perceiving and controlling every shift in direction, every curve, and every pause. One could say that it is this very presence in motion that fully manifests the meaning and essence of creativity.

Picture 7: Direction of Movement

As it moves, the pen creates both black and white spaces, generates various rhythms, and, through shifts in its angle, produces contrasts of thickness and thinness. Within this movement, delicate proportions between lines emerge, born from a fusion of skill, intuition, and the calligrapher’s moment-to-moment awareness.

Proportions in Movements

Concepts such as the golden ratio and classical geometric structures offer valuable tools for explaining and better understanding the proportions within letters, words, and calligraphic compositions. Structurally, this is considered one of the most complex and challenging aspects of the art of calligraphy. Achieving such proportions not only requires years of dedicated practice, but also demands a profound sense of aesthetics and an intuitive understanding of the visual relationships between the elements of a word.

Types of Rhythms in Movements

Rhythm plays a role not only in dimensions (length, width, and thickness), but also in angles, repetition, and in the creation of black and white space. As shown in the image, these rhythms are present on multiple levels, from the formation of a single letter to the structure of a word, and ultimately throughout the entire composition. The precise interplay of these rhythms leads to the creation of a cohesive and dynamic harmony, which is one of the defining features of a true work of art.

End of the Line, Beginning of Vision

The art of calligraphy goes beyond beautiful writing, it's a journey inward. A journey in which proportions, angles, rhythms, and rules are merely tools to express something deeper: the conscious soul of the artist. Every dot and every stroke of the pen echoes perception, intuition, and a profound presence in the moment.

Although learning technical principles and visual rules is essential for shaping this art, what truly brings the line to life is the human presence behind it—the person who asks: “Who am I? And why do I write?”
And perhaps that very question is the beginning of writing a line that is not only beautiful, but alive.
